Introduction

Chemical industry is a vast industry that incorporates all different types of product producing industries whose generation is based on heavy use of chemicals. Usually, industries that are involved with industrial chemical generation are broadly known as chemical industry. As per the end-products, processes or raw materials associated with chemical industries, such industry can be classified into several classes. Understanding the huge diversity of chemical producing industries, such chemical industries are usually categorized under broad categories such as inorganic and organic chemical producing industries, fertilizer industries, refining and petroleum industries, electroplating and heat treatment industries, pesticide industries, hydro generated oil/soap industries, and so on (Soni, 2020). Along with these, sometimes pharmaceutical industries, agricultural product processing industries, chemical processing/supplying industries, etc. are also regarded as specialized chemical industries. Thus, chemical industry covers a variety of industries which are focused on producing chemicals of different purposes. Understanding these varieties of classes under chemical industry is important, since such clustering helps to design suitable policies, initiatives, and interventions more readily (Banerjee et al., 2003). The variety and extent of such chemical industries are more diverse especially in the developed countries where there are adequate opportunities for industrial growth (Arora et al., 2001). The developing countries are not much focused on the growth of their chemical industries since they are already struggle to meet the basic demands of their citizen and to maintain a sustainable economic condition. All these different types of industrial inclusions make chemical industry a vast industry incorporating investment and supply chain management of multiple different types of industries. This is why investment and compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of the chemical industries are often higher than many other industries. The commonly considered subcategories of chemical industry is expected to reach to a worth of 953.9 billion of USD by 2027 at a 5% CAGR from the 873.38 billion of USD worth market of 2019 (Allied Market Research, 2020; BCC Research, 2020). Though, the growth percentage might not seem that high, its corresponding monetary value of growth is extremely high and can regulate the growth of the country's economy tremendously. Even in 2016 and 2017, the CAGR of the chemical industry's total performance shareholder returns (TSR) experienced over 24% CAGR (McKinsey). This percentage of CAGR is significantly high considering the wide extent of multiple sectors of chemical industries. Unfortunately, many of such chemical industries are not much focused on their environmental sustainability; instead they are more focused on profit making. In this regard, Domazlicky & Weber (2004) argue that economic growth without enough initiatives of environmental protection wonâ€™t be sustainable and such unsustainability will lead to comparatively higher expenditure in the long run. Being one of the largest cluster of different types of product producing industries, chemical industries cause generation of different types of waste, most of which are highly hazardous chemical wastes and such wastes are responsible for causing environmental unsustainability. It is because most of such chemical wastes are not biodegradable and contain highly toxic chemicals. Besides, since such chemical industry produced wastes are generated under certain extreme physical or chemical conditions (i.e., high pressure, high temperature, concentrated acidic, or alkaline conditions, etc.), handling such waste sustainably is a huge concern. Improper handling and maintenance of these highly toxic wastes may bring about degradation of health or environmental conditions. Additionally, proper management or treatment of chemical wastes is also quite expensive, as such management initiatives often require sophisticated technological and technical set-up. Considering corresponding economic, technical, and hazard-related concerns of chemical industry produced waste, pregeneration waste minimization, known as source-level waste minimization, can be preferred over postgeneration treatment or management initiatives for chemical industry produced waste (Chaaban, 2001). Thus, source reduction and waste minimization approaches can be considered as source level chemical waste minimization initiatives. This chapter explores and categorizes some of the major classes of waste produced in different chemical industries. At the same time, it discusses on some of the major source-level waste minimization initiatives that can be adopted by the chemical industries.
